Backyard gardening and Water reuse

Backyard gardening simply refers to the growing of crops in a home back garden aimed at supplying the family with mainly greens and vegetables regularly; the surplus is sold off to neighbors.

For urban areas, the practice is now specialized to urban gardening where people grow these crops including greens, vegetables, fruits, herbs and spices in urban and peri-urban areas where having little or no land is no longer a hindrance for urban households to engage in agriculture. These gardens are usually maintained all year round i.e. during both the dry and wet seasons; it is the crops grown that may be varied from time to time.

The practice has in addition been expanded to urban agriculture to also include the rearing of chickens, ducks, and fish.


Urban gardening is now done in both front and back yards, on verandas of houses, on balconies and on rooftops. It now comes in very many forms. Sacks, old tires, plastic bottles, plastic pipes, containers, buckets, old gumboots; you name it, are being used to grow crops.

Urban gardening is influenced by a number of factors including the space one has available, the types of plants to be grown, and the capital requirements.

Urban agriculture not only ensures that the family has access to healthy foods thus securing the family’s food and nutrition security, but it can also improve the household’s income when the surplus produce is sold off thus stimulating the local economy.


During dry periods, crops grown under urban gardening need to be watered daily. In most cases, they are watered twice in a day, early morning and in the evening. This increases the water demand for the household. Water availability varies across households in Uganda. Many households in urban areas spend 500 to 1000 UGX on a jerrican of water for their domestic needs. As such, the cost of water will also influence the magnitude of one’s investment in urban gardening. Water therefore needs to be used efficiently in the garden in order to minimize water costs.


One way of using water more efficiently in a household engaging in urban gardening is to reuse water. Pre-used water, that does not contain soap, oil, salt or any other additives, can be used to water urban gardens instead of using fresh water. Water that is used to rinse clothes and dishes can be used. Also, water that is used during cooking can be used. This can include for instance, water used to wash vegetables, fruits and other foods before eating or cooking them, water used to rinse rice before cooking it, and water used to boil foods like eggs, potatoes etc. after it cools. After this water cools down, it is recommended to use it within 24 hours before it develops bacteria. Also, pre-used water can be diluted with freshwater before watering urban gardens.
